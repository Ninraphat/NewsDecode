Shocking Twist in Nong Angoon Case: 13-Year-Old Stepsister and 17-Year-Old Uncle Arrested for Suffocating and Concealing Body of Seven-Year-Old Kanchanaburi Girl

A week-long investigation into the death of seven-year-old Kasama, widely known as "Nong Angoon," took a devastating and unexpected turn on Saturday when police in Sangkhla Buri district, Kanchanaburi, arrested the victim's own 13-year-old stepsister, Kanda, and her 17-year-old uncle, Pae, after both allegedly confessed to their roles in causing the child's death and concealing her body.

Police said the incident occurred on the afternoon of 30 May, when the two girls were playing together at their home in Ban Thi Khrong. According to Kanda's statement to investigators, an argument broke out between the two children over a shared toy and use of a mobile phone. During the altercation, Kanda allegedly covered the younger girl's mouth and nose, causing her to suffocate. The two girls shared the same mother but had different fathers. Their mother told police the pair had frequently quarrelled and fought over the phone.

After realising what had happened, Kanda panicked and summoned her 17-year-old uncle Pae to help her. Together, the two minors concealed the body in a wardrobe inside the house. Several hours later, they transferred the body to a motorcycle fitted with a sidecar and drove to a rubber plantation near the Chao Pho Khao Yai shrine, where they disposed of the body beneath a bamboo grove before returning home and behaving as if nothing had occurred.

On Saturday, Pol Lt Gen Pisit Tanprasert, chief of Provincial Police Region 7, confirmed the arrests and said the 17-year-old had been escorted to re-enact the crime at two locations — the victim's home and the disposal site. The autopsy conducted at Loei Hospital confirmed the cause of death as respiratory and circulatory failure, consistent with suffocation. Earlier preliminary examinations had indicated possible signs of sexual assault, but investigators have not yet formally confirmed this allegation.

Over the course of the investigation, police had questioned more than 40 individuals, including three Buddhist monks and several local residents, and had collected over 25 DNA samples. The earlier suspicion had focused on adults in the community, and the case had attracted senior police oversight including a visit by the deputy national police chief. The revelation that two juvenile family members were responsible came as a profound shock to the community and to the victim's parents.

The victim's father, who no longer lived with the family, said he found the account difficult to accept, questioning whether a dispute over a toy or phone could truly have led to his daughter's death. The mother was reported to be in severe distress and was receiving mental health counselling. Relatives urged the public to avoid speculating online about the case.

Both suspects are juveniles and are currently under the supervision of the Kanchanaburi Juvenile and Family Court. Under Thai law, minors cannot be tried as adults and must be processed through the juvenile justice system. Investigators said the case would continue as they sought to establish the full sequence of events and determine whether additional charges were warranted.
