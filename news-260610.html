Pattaya Arsenal Case Expands: Chinese Suspect Sun Mingchen Linked to Transnational Scam Networks, Thai Navy Officers Charged with Illegal Arms Supply

A routine traffic accident in Chon Buri province has unravelled one of Thailand's most serious national security cases in recent years, leading to the arrest of 31-year-old Chinese national Sun Mingchen and the subsequent detention of at least 11 additional suspects across seven interconnected criminal investigations involving illegal weapons trafficking, cyberscam networks, money laundering, and the fraudulent procurement of official Thai identity documents.

The chain of events began on the evening of 8 May, when police were alerted to a vehicle rollover accident in Bang Lamung district, Chon Buri. The driver, Sun Mingchen, and his Taiwanese passenger, Ma Yu-Hsin, 33, were uninjured. However, a subsequent search of the vehicle uncovered firearms and ammunition. A further search of Sun's rented residence near Pattaya revealed a cache of military-grade weapons, including M16 and M4 assault rifles, a Glock 26 handgun, fragmentation grenades, C4 explosives, anti-personnel devices, bulletproof vests, and four 20-litre containers of fuel. Video found on his mobile phone showed him training a machine gun on a target.

Sun was initially charged with multiple offences: possessing firearms and ammunition without permission, carrying a firearm in a public place, possessing military-grade explosives, and holding unlicensed firearms. Ma Yu-Hsin was also charged with jointly possessing firearms. Sun reportedly confessed to all charges and claimed he had been suffering from depression and intended the weapons for a suicide bombing, though psychiatric evaluation was ordered to verify this claim.

Further investigation revealed that Sun held both Chinese and Cambodian passports, as well as a pink identification card issued to long-term foreign residents of Thailand. He had first entered Thailand in 2020 on a tourist visa and had re-entered on 27 January 2026 under a multi-year visa permit. He was also registered at an address in Bangkok's Khlong Sam Wa district and had been paying 38,000 baht per month for his Pattaya rental.

The probe rapidly expanded under orders from national police chief Pol Gen Kittharath Punpetch. Five Thai suspects — including navy officers and civilians — were charged with the illegal possession and sale of weapons of war after investigators traced the arms supply chain back to a Pattaya shooting range. Sun had met shooting range operator Kachen Sianglum at the facility and exchanged contact details. Three suspects were arrested and two others were summoned for questioning. All denied the charges and stated they would prove their innocence in court.

Police also uncovered strong financial links. Authorities confirmed Sun allegedly acted as a financial "wallet" for scam syndicates based in neighbouring countries, particularly Cambodia, with international money transfers involving tens of millions of baht traced through connected accounts. A separate sub-case involved the fraudulent application for Sun's pink ID card, which led to the arrest of a former district official in Chiang Mai.

The case prompted Prime Minister Anutin Charnvirakul to summon senior police officers to Government House for urgent discussions and has led to calls for stricter immigration screening and a review of long-term visa privileges available to wealthy foreign nationals. Police said the investigation reflects broader vulnerabilities in Thailand's status as an international tourism and business hub, and that surveillance operations had been expanded to tourist destinations including Koh Phangan, Phuket, Pai, and Hua Hin.
