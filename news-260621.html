48 Arrested in Major Southern Thailand Nominee Land Crackdown; Israelis, Europeans Among Foreign Suspects Charged with Illegal Land Ownership

Thai authorities have arrested 48 suspects — 27 Thai nationals and 21 foreign nationals — following a large-scale crackdown on illegal land nominee arrangements across Phuket, Phang Nga, and Krabi provinces. The operation, targeting networks in which foreigners use Thai companies as nominees to hold land that foreign nationals are legally prohibited from owning directly, resulted in the execution of 55 out of 59 court-approved arrest warrants and an additional 29 search warrants.

In Phuket, investigators identified two categories of illegal land ownership. The first involved ten companies suspected of acting as nominees for foreign investors, holding four land plots covering more than two rai, valued at 116 million baht. Officers arrested nine foreign nationals, including five Israelis, two French nationals, one Dutch national, and one Russian. The second category involved 39 companies that owned land despite having foreign shareholders holding more than half of the shares — a clear violation of the Land Code.

In Phang Nga, six arrest warrants and three search warrants were approved. A British national was arrested in connection with a network that included the Sava Beach Hotel, where seven villas were found to be rented out on a daily basis despite the property lacking a hotel licence. Investigators also identified a company with a foreign-majority shareholding structure holding a nine-rai plot of land worth 54 million baht, and filed a complaint with the Takua Pa land office.

In Krabi, 40 arrest warrants and 13 search warrants were issued. Officers executed 39 and arrested 38 suspects — 27 Thais and 11 foreigners, comprising six Israelis, two Polish, two Swiss, and one South African national. One of the most significant cases involved Tropical House Co, a property development company linked to Polish investors that had registered capital of just 4 million baht but controlled 16 plots covering more than six rai, valued at around 200 million baht.

A common pattern identified across all three provinces was the use of Thai nationals with social security cards but no apparent financial capacity to serve as nominee shareholders — often employees or relatives of company directors. Authorities noted that many had been used to circumvent Thai law, which restricts direct foreign land ownership to certain limited categories. Officials said the operation marked a significant step in enforcing long-neglected provisions of the Land Code and vowed to continue prosecuting both foreign investors and the Thai nominees who facilitate such arrangements.
