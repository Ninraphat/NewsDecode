Two Arrested for Selling "Premium Grade" Mule Accounts Linked to Cybercrime Suppression Law Violations

Police arrested two suspects in the Pak Kret area of Nonthaburi province after they were caught in the act of delivering a bank account being sold as a "premium grade" mule account, part of a wider crackdown on financial networks that facilitate online scams in Thailand.

The operation began after investigators received a tip about an individual advertising the sale of bank accounts on an online platform, describing them using the term "premium grade" — a label typically used to mean the account had a high transaction limit and clean transaction history, making it more valuable to criminal networks because it could process larger sums without triggering bank fraud alerts.

Undercover officers posed as buyers and arranged a meeting to collect the account materials. When the suspect, identified as Worawut, arrived to hand over an envelope containing a bank passbook, ATM card, and PIN details, officers moved in and made the arrest. During questioning, Worawut told police he was not the account owner and had simply been paid 10,000 baht to deliver the envelope on someone else's behalf.

Investigators said the account had been supplied through a chain of intermediaries linked to a larger mule-account network. Police explained that mule accounts are commonly used to receive money from scam victims and launder the proceeds of illegal activities, making financial transactions more difficult to trace back to the original perpetrators. Such accounts form a critical link in the infrastructure that allows call-centre gangs and online fraud syndicates to move money quickly across multiple accounts before victims realise they have been scammed.

Both suspects were charged with jointly procuring, advertising, or facilitating the sale, rental, or lending of bank accounts and electronic payment accounts, in violation of Thailand's Cybercrime Suppression Act. They were handed over to Pak Kret police station for further legal action and were being held in custody while the investigation continued. Police said they would trace the chain of intermediaries to identify the original supplier of the account and determine whether it had already been used in any completed fraud cases.

The case highlights the ongoing challenge Thai authorities face in combating the trade in mule accounts, which remains a key enabler of the billions of baht in losses Thai citizens suffer annually from online scams, particularly those operated by transnational criminal syndicates based in neighbouring countries.
