After 11 Years, Thai Court Sentences Two Uyghur Men to Death for 2015 Erawan Shrine Bombing That Killed 20

The Bangkok South Criminal Court on Thursday delivered a long-awaited verdict in Thailand's deadliest bombing case, sentencing two ethnic Uyghur men from China's Xinjiang region to death for carrying out the August 2015 attack on the Erawan Shrine, a popular tourist site at Bangkok's Ratchaprasong intersection.

A four-judge panel convicted Bilal Mohammed, 41 — also known by the alias Adem Karadag — and Mieraili Yusufu, 36, of premeditated murder and attempted murder, ruling that their actions amounted to terrorism and imposing the maximum punishment available under Thai law. The court found that on the evening of 17 August 2015, Mieraili secretly handed a concealed explosive device to Mohammed, who then placed the bomb inside the crowded shrine area before walking away. Shortly afterward, Mieraili detonated the device, triggering an explosion that killed 20 people — six Thai citizens and 14 foreign nationals — and injured more than 120 others.

Prosecutors reconstructed the men's movements using CCTV footage, witness testimony, and forensic evidence. The court ruled that the defendants committed acts that violated multiple laws, and therefore imposed the death sentence — the harshest penalty available. Both men were also fined 1,000 baht each for carrying weapons in a public place, and ordered to pay more than one million baht in damages.

The case had taken more than a decade to reach a final verdict. It began in a military court in 2016 before being transferred to a civilian court. The lengthy delay was attributed to multiple factors, including difficulties finding a Uyghur-speaking interpreter acceptable to both parties — the men rejected translators offered by the Chinese embassy. The defendants claimed they had suffered mistreatment and torture while in detention and that their confessions had been coerced. However, the judges ruled there was no evidence of torture and that investigators did not appear to have coerced the confessions.

Following the verdict, Mieraili reacted angrily, stating he did not accept any of it. Defence lawyers said the men would appeal the ruling, arguing that many aspects of the case had not been fully considered, including how the defendants were treated during the proceedings. International human rights organisations have previously criticised the unusually lengthy duration of the trial.

A third suspect had been apprehended in 2017 but was acquitted in 2024. Police had originally named 17 suspects in connection with the attack, but only Mohammed and Mieraili were ever apprehended. Beijing welcomed the outcome, with officials describing the attackers as totally inhumane and extremely heinous.
