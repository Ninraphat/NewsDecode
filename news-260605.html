Forensic Doctors Rule Out Knife Attack on Former F1 Driver Mika Salo in Bangkok; Police Conclude Wound Was Accidental

Thai police and forensic specialists have concluded that a deep leg wound sustained by Finnish former Formula One driver Mika Salo near Bangkok's Nana intersection on Sukhumvit Road was most likely caused by a blunt impact during a motorcycle-related accident, not a deliberate knife attack as initially alleged by international media.

Salo, 59, first noticed he was bleeding only after a passerby alerted him approximately 50 metres from where the incident occurred on the evening of 19 May. He was unaware of any injury at the time, describing the sensation as a "small bump." He subsequently took a taxi to Bumrungrad International Hospital, where doctors found a deep open wound with torn muscle tissue, requiring eight sutures in the muscle and 20 more to close the skin, totalling 28 stitches.

Hospital staff reportedly told Salo that several other patients had arrived that same evening with similar injuries, raising early speculation that a motorcyclist may have been deliberately attacking pedestrians. International and Finnish media seized on these claims, with one British outlet describing the incident as a "stabbing rampage" in Bangkok. The viral reports triggered intense global concern over tourist safety in Thailand and prompted an urgent investigation by the Royal Thai Police.

National Police Chief Pol Gen Kitrat Phanphet ordered the Metropolitan Police Bureau to accelerate the investigation, review CCTV footage, question witnesses, and publicly present findings. Lt Gen Trairong Phiwphan, spokesman for the Royal Thai Police, visited Salo at the Police General Hospital, where forensic doctors conducted a detailed examination of the healed wound.

Forensic specialists found that the wound displayed irregular edges rather than the smooth, clean cuts typically associated with blade lacerations. Doctors therefore concluded the injury was inconsistent with a knife attack and more likely resulted from a motorcycle licence plate or similar blunt object. Their findings directly contradicted earlier hospital speculation and widespread media reports.

Salo himself later downplayed the episode, telling reporters he did not believe he had been deliberately attacked. Despite the ordeal, he expressed continued confidence in Thailand's safety and said he planned to return. However, the controversy caused significant reputational damage, arriving at a sensitive time as Thailand lobbies actively for a future Formula One Grand Prix race.

Police confirmed that Salo had not formally filed a complaint and said the case remained open pending further review of CCTV evidence. Authorities acknowledged the case had placed Thailand's tourism safety image under international scrutiny.
