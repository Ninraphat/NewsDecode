Extortion Claims Against Deputy PM Phiphat Spark Political Controversy; Sondhi Limthongkul Threatens Legal Action After Defamation Prison Term

Two overlapping legal and political controversies dominated Thai headlines on Wednesday, both involving veteran media personality Sondhi Limthongkul and touching on allegations of extortion, defamation, and the boundaries of press freedom in Thailand.

The first controversy erupted on social media when a Facebook user identified as "Joe Montanee" alleged that a group of individuals had attempted to pressure Deputy Prime Minister and Transport Minister Phiphat Ratchakitprakarn into paying 5 million baht per month by threatening to dig up and publish damaging information about him and affiliated businesses within the PT Group. The alleged scheme reportedly failed. Political activist and former journalist Nuttha "Bow" Mahattana added fuel to the fire by writing on her own Facebook page that at least one prominent Thai media outlet was widely known within media circles for engaging in extortion, though she did not name the organisation directly.

The allegation drew a cryptic comment from Prime Minister Anutin Charnvirakul's official social media account, which responded to Mr Phiphat's post with the words "thank you for providing another perspective." When questioned by reporters at Government House, Mr Anutin smiled and said he did not know what they were referring to, declining to confirm whether he had personally written the comment or whether it had been posted by a social media administrator. The vague response further stoked public speculation about political undercurrents behind the affair. No formal complaint has been filed and no government agency has yet verified the extortion claim.

In a directly related development, Sondhi Limthongkul, 78, founder of Manager Media Group and veteran leader of the People's Alliance for Democracy, threatened to take legal action after the Criminal Court on Tuesday sentenced him to four months in prison without suspension for defaming former MP Thanakorn Nanti. The court found Sondhi guilty under Section 328 of the Criminal Code, which governs defamation by publication, after he made a series of statements on his programme between June and September 2023 linking Mr Thanakorn to corruption in five energy-related projects.

The court ordered Sondhi to pay 2 million baht in damages, 20,000 baht in legal fees, and to publish the court ruling in seven newspapers at his own expense. Mr Thanakorn had originally sought 50 million baht in restitution. In response, Sondhi said he disputed the verdict and would file a counter-lawsuit against those he believed had orchestrated the case against him.

The two cases, while legally distinct, have converged in public discussion around the role of media power in Thai politics, the use of defamation law as a political weapon, and the accountability of public figures who use media platforms to make unverified allegations against political opponents. Both cases are expected to continue generating political fallout in the weeks ahead.
