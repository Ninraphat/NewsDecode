Koh Samui Taxi Mafia Murder: Nine Suspects Arrested, Three Still Fugitives

Police have apprehended nine of twelve suspects in the assault and murder of a volunteer taxi driver on the tourist island of Koh Samui, Surat Thani province. The victim, Sikkharin Phromcharoen, 31, was beaten and shot dead on May 24 in a dispute over passenger pick-up zones involving illegal black-plate taxi drivers.

Sikkharin was widely admired on the island for providing free transport to pregnant women, elderly residents, disabled individuals and poor families. His tragic death sparked public outrage across Thailand.

According to his wife, Onchuma, the conflict erupted after Sikkharin parked in an area claimed by a group of rival, unlicensed drivers. A local mediator had previously intervened to resolve the dispute, and the family believed tensions had eased before the fatal attack.

Pol Col Kritnithi Thongthip, spokesman for Surat Thani police, confirmed that detectives had gathered sufficient evidence to obtain court warrants for all twelve suspects. The apprehended suspects face charges of premeditated murder and illegal possession and use of firearms. Court approval was also granted to extend the detention of two suspects pending further interrogation.

The first suspect to be arrested, Jatupol Ruangkong, 42, surrendered to officers at Bo Phut police station after a warrant was issued by Koh Samui Provincial Court. He denied direct involvement in the assault and shooting, claiming he merely knocked on the victim's car window and that several men then confronted the victim before gunshots were fired. He said he worked as a security guard at a nearby nightclub.

The victim's family, fearing retaliation from influential local groups, fled Surat Thani with more than ten relatives and entered a police witness protection programme. Relatives had not yet collected Sikkharin's body for funeral rites due to ongoing safety concerns.

Police asked the public to submit any information on the whereabouts of the three remaining fugitives. Authorities denied the existence of an organised taxi mafia on Koh Samui but acknowledged that the murder was part of a deeper, long-standing territorial conflict over lucrative transport routes on the island.
