Thailand Dismantles Massive Scam Network: 30,000 Suspects Arrested, 1,450 Proxy Firms Shuttered, 24 Billion Baht in Assets Seized in Nine-Month Crackdown

Thai authorities have announced the results of a sweeping nine-month anti-fraud campaign, confirming the arrest of approximately 30,000 scammers — including around 70 gang leaders — and the dismantling of 1,450 proxy companies used to launder proceeds and conceal the true ownership of criminal operations. The total value of seized assets and cash reached approximately 24 billion baht, making it one of the largest coordinated law enforcement actions against cybercrime in Southeast Asian history.

The Anti Cyber Scam Centre (ACSC) spearheaded the initiative, operating a real-time war room that intervened to save 862 people from active scam syndicates before funds could be transferred. Over the course of the campaign, authorities suspended more than 300,000 phone numbers linked to scammers, resulting in a 77.94% plunge in monthly scam calls. Additionally, 122,840 fraudulent URLs were blocked, with 108,517 of those originating on Facebook.

For proxy business offences — a key mechanism by which foreign criminal networks disguise their operations behind Thai-registered entities — police obtained court arrest warrants for 29 Thais and 67 foreigners, and apprehended 24 Thais and 41 foreigners. Those detained included nationals of Israel, France, Russia, Poland, Switzerland, South Africa, and Britain, underscoring the transnational character of the networks being dismantled.

The government also repatriated 1,862 Thai nationals who had been trafficked or coerced into working in scam compounds outside the country, with 67.9% returning from Cambodia and 32.1% from Laos. A further 2,489 Thais were prosecuted for illegally crossing borders in connection with 1,031 scam cases. Authorities deported 2,647 foreigners who had infiltrated Thailand following crackdowns on scam compounds in the KK Park and Minletpan areas of neighbouring Myanmar, and identified roughly 60 suspected scam locations in Cambodia, Laos, and Myanmar.

Police warned that criminal networks are growing more sophisticated, increasingly using artificial intelligence, cryptocurrency, and complex transaction layering to evade asset seizure. Authorities urged the public to follow the principle of "Don't believe, don't rush, don't transfer" when dealing with unsolicited calls or messages, and to verify all claims through official government platforms before taking any financial action.
