Thai Woman Faces Myanmar Trial Linked to U.S. Diplomat Killing; Immigration Hearing Opens as Murder Charge Carries Possible Death Penalty

A Thai woman, Pavinee Supasirivisan, appeared before a court in Yangon this week in a case linked to the killing of a U.S. diplomat. The hearing focused first on an immigration-related charge, but she is also facing a separate murder charge connected to the diplomat's death in May. Lawyers familiar with the case said the proceedings took place at Kamayut Township Court, where prosecution witnesses, including immigration officers, gave evidence.

The case has drawn attention because the alleged victim was a U.S. Embassy employee in Myanmar and because the court process is unfolding under Myanmar's military-run government. Journalists are generally not allowed to attend many court proceedings there, and officials have released very few details. It remains unclear whether Pavinee has entered a plea, how long the current trial will last, or when the murder case itself will be heard.

According to reporting cited by AP, the diplomat was found dead on May 11 at the Sakura Residence & Hotel in Yangon, a long-stay facility used by diplomats, business people, and other foreign visitors. The hotel is located about 1.5 kilometres from the U.S. Embassy. Lawyers familiar with the matter said the victim had suffered stab wounds to the head and neck. The U.S. State Department confirmed the death of a government employee but did not release the diplomat's name in its earlier statements.

The immigration charge reportedly applies to foreign nationals accused of committing a crime in Myanmar and can carry a sentence of six months to five years. The murder charge is far more serious: if convicted, Pavinee could face a prison term ranging from ten years to the death penalty. The Times later identified the diplomat as Daniel Riva and described Pavinee as his former wife, adding another layer of public interest to the case.

Thailand's Foreign Ministry has confirmed that it is providing consular assistance to Pavinee but has declined to give more information. The limited public record means many key facts remain undisclosed, including the exact status of the murder trial, whether foreign observers will be present, and what evidence prosecutors plan to use. For now, the case shows how a cross-border criminal allegation can become complicated when it involves diplomatic staff, foreign defendants, and a court system operating under tight political control.
