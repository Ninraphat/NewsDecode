Thailand's "Michael Jackson" Jailed for Two Years over Fatal Drunk-Driving Crash; Court Convicts Singer Tik Shiro of Vehicular Homicide After Guilty Plea

The Min Buri Criminal Court in Bangkok handed down a two-year prison sentence on Wednesday to Manasawin Nantasen, 64, the veteran pop singer better known by his stage name Tik Shiro, after finding him guilty of drunk driving resulting in loss of life. The court initially imposed a four-year term but reduced it to two years after the defendant entered a guilty plea and provided testimony that the court deemed beneficial to the case. His driver's licence was also revoked.

The incident that led to the conviction occurred on the night of October 10, 2024, when Manasawin was driving across a bridge in Bangkok's Sai Mai district while intoxicated. His vehicle struck a stationary motorbike, killing Thianporn, a 28-year-old woman who was riding it. Her younger brother Chakraphat, 21, was knocked off the bridge in the collision and sustained critical injuries. He was hospitalised but ultimately succumbed to his wounds on March 18, 2025 — more than five months after the crash. The deaths of both siblings were attributed to the accused.

Video footage of Manasawin kneeling at the accident scene in apparent grief went viral on Thai social media and drew widespread public attention. During the trial, he paid 3.5 million baht in compensation to the victims' family and demonstrated remorse by attending their funeral rites. The court took both factors into account when handing down the reduced sentence, noting that the defendant had shown a cooperative disposition throughout the proceedings.

Manasawin rose to national fame in the late 1980s and 1990s with his trademark fedora hat, leather jackets, and moonwalk-inspired dance moves, which earned him widespread comparisons to global pop icon Michael Jackson. His sentencing has reignited debate in Thailand about accountability for celebrities and influential figures within the justice system. On the same day, rock musician Sek Loso was released from custody after serving just over a year for drug possession and illegal firearm offences.

Thailand has long struggled with impunity in high-profile cases. The country ranks ninth among 175 WHO member states for road traffic fatalities, and critics point to past cases — such as that of Red Bull heir Vorayuth Yoovidhya, who was never charged despite being implicated in a fatal hit-and-run — as evidence that wealth and status can shield perpetrators from prosecution. Advocates for road safety welcomed the verdict as a signal that even prominent figures are not exempt from the law.
